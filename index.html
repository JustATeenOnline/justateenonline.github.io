Modern Periodic Table — Structure

🗂️ Structure of the Modern Periodic Table

  • Periods (rows): 7 horizontal rows. Period number = number of electron shells. Period 1: 2 elements. Period 2 & 3: 8 each. Period 4 & 5: 18 each. Period 6: 32.
  • Groups (columns): 18 vertical columns. Elements in same group have same number of valence electrons → similar chemical properties.
  • Total elements: 118 confirmed.
  • Metals: Left and centre of table (~80% of elements)
  • Non-metals: Top-right corner
  • Metalloids (semimetals): Along the staircase diagonal (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te)
  • Noble gases: Group 18 (Group 0), rightmost column — completely filled shells

Electronic Configuration & Valency

⚛️ Electronic Configuration — Key Points

  • Shell capacity: Shell n holds max 2n² electrons. K(n=1): 2, L(n=2): 8, M(n=3): 18, N(n=4): 32.
  • Valence electrons: Electrons in the outermost (valence) shell. Determines chemical behaviour.
  • Valency: For metals = number of valence electrons. For non-metals = 8 − valence electrons (tendency to gain/share to complete octet).
  • Period number = number of occupied electron shells.
  • Group number = number of valence electrons (for s and p block).

Characteristics of Periods & Groups

↔️ Characteristics of a Period

  • Same number of electron shells
  • Properties change gradually from metallic to non-metallic (left to right)
  • Atomic radius decreases
  • Valency first increases (1→4) then decreases (4→0)
  • Ionisation energy generally increases
  • Elements change from strong bases (Na) → amphoteric (Al) → strong acids (Cl)

↕️ Characteristics of a Group

  • Same number of valence electrons
  • Same valency → similar chemical properties
  • Atomic radius increases down the group
  • Ionisation energy decreases down
  • Metallic character increases down
  • Reactivity: metals increase ↓; non-metals decrease ↓
Exam Q & A
Q1. State the Modern Periodic Law. How did Moseley improve upon Mendeleev's periodic law?+
Modern Periodic Law (Moseley, 1913): The physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic number.

Improvement over Mendeleev:
1. Mendeleev arranged by atomic mass → anomalies (e.g., Co before Ni despite higher mass, Te before I). Moseley arranged by atomic number → all anomalies resolved.
2. Atomic number is a more fundamental property (number of protons). Atomic mass can vary for isotopes.
3. Position of isotopes was clarified — same Z, same position despite different masses.
Q2. An element X has atomic number 16. State its: (a) period, (b) group, (c) valency, (d) metallic/non-metallic nature, (e) type of oxide formed.+
Element with Z=16 is Sulphur (S), configuration: 2,8,6.

(a) Period 3 (3 electron shells)
(b) Group 16 (6 valence electrons)
(c) Valency = 2 (8−6=2, tendency to gain 2 electrons)
(d) Non-metallic (right side of period 3, more than 4 valence electrons)
(e) Acidic oxide (SO₂, SO₃) — non-metals form acidic oxides that dissolve in water to form acids (H₂SO₃, H₂SO₄)
Q3. Why do elements in the same group have similar chemical properties?+
Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons in their outermost shell. Chemical properties of an element are determined by its valence electrons — they participate in bond formation, determine valency, and govern reactivity.

Since all elements in a group have identical valence electron count, they form similar types of compounds, have the same valency, and show similar reactions. For example, all alkali metals (Group 1) have 1 valence electron → valency 1 → form similar oxides (Na₂O, K₂O), hydroxides (NaOH, KOH), and chlorides (NaCl, KCl).
Q4. Compare the properties of halogens (Group 17) as you move down the group.+
Down Group 17 (F → Cl → Br → I):

Physical state: F₂ (gas) → Cl₂ (gas) → Br₂ (liquid) → I₂ (solid) — boiling point increases down.
Colour: F₂ (pale yellow) → Cl₂ (yellow-green) → Br₂ (reddish-brown) → I₂ (violet)
Reactivity: Decreases F > Cl > Br > I (electron affinity decreases; F is most reactive non-metal)
Electronegativity: Decreases down (F = 4.0, highest of all elements)
Atomic radius: Increases (more shells)
Oxidising power: Decreases (F strongest oxidising agent)

Trends: Atomic radius ↑ down a group & right→left across a period. IE, EN, EA ↑ up a group & left→right across a period.

Chemical Bonding — Ionic, Covalent & Coordinate

Octet Rule & Why Atoms Bond

⚛️ Octet Rule (Lewis)

Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a stable configuration of 8 electrons in the outermost shell (like the nearest noble gas). Hydrogen is an exception — it needs only 2 (duplet).

  • Ions formed: When electron transfer is more energetically favourable (large electronegativity difference, usually metal + non-metal)
  • Covalent bonds: When sharing is preferred (small or zero electronegativity difference, usually non-metal + non-metal)
Ionic (Electrovalent) Bond

⚡ Ionic Bond — Definition & Formation

An ionic bond is formed by the complete transfer of one or more electrons from a metal atom to a non-metal atom, resulting in the formation of oppositely charged ions which are held together by electrostatic attraction.

  • Formed between: Metal (low IE, loses electrons → cation) and non-metal (high EA, gains electrons → anion)
  • Electronegativity difference > 1.7 generally indicates ionic bond
  • The ions arrange in a crystal lattice — each ion surrounded by oppositely charged ions

📝 Formation of NaCl (Sodium Chloride)

  • Na (2,8,1) → loses 1 electron → Na⁺ (2,8) — configuration of Ne
  • Cl (2,8,7) → gains 1 electron → Cl⁻ (2,8,8) — configuration of Ar
  • Na⁺ and Cl⁻ attract each other → ionic bond → NaCl
Electron TransferNa → Na⁺ + e⁻
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻
Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl

📝 Formation of MgO (Magnesium Oxide)

  • Mg (2,8,2) → loses 2 electrons → Mg²⁺ (2,8)
  • O (2,6) → gains 2 electrons → O²⁻ (2,8)
  • Mg²⁺ + O²⁻ → MgO (ionic bond)

Covalent Bond

🔗 Covalent Bond — Definition & Formation

A covalent bond is formed by the mutual sharing of one or more pairs of electrons between two atoms, both of which contribute electrons to the shared pair. Each shared pair = one bond.

  • Single bond: 1 shared pair (e.g., H–H, H–Cl, Cl–Cl)
  • Double bond: 2 shared pairs (e.g., O=O, C=O, C=C)
  • Triple bond: 3 shared pairs (e.g., N≡N, C≡C, H–C≡N)

Coordinate (Dative) Bond

🎯 Coordinate (Dative Covalent) Bond

A coordinate bond is a type of covalent bond in which both electrons of the shared pair are donated by one atom alone (the donor), to the other atom (the acceptor). Once formed, it is identical to a regular covalent bond.

  • Donor: atom with a lone pair to donate (e.g., N in NH₃, O in H₂O)
  • Acceptor: atom with an empty orbital or electron deficiency (e.g., H⁺, BF₃)
  • Represented by an arrow: donor → acceptor

NH₃ + HCl → NH₄⁺Cl⁻ (Ammonium chloride):
The lone pair on N in NH₃ is donated to the H⁺ from HCl → N → H coordinate bond forms → NH₄⁺ ion (4 equivalent N–H bonds, one was coordinate)

H₂O + H⁺ → H₃O⁺ (Hydronium/Oxonium ion):
Lone pair on O in H₂O donated to H⁺ → O → H coordinate bond → H₃O⁺

Electrovalency & Covalency
ElectrovalencyThe number of electrons an atom loses, gains, or transfers during ionic bond formation. Na: +1, Mg: +2, Al: +3, Cl: −1, O: −2, N: −3.
CovalencyThe number of electron pairs an atom shares in forming covalent bonds. H: 1, O: 2, N: 3, C: 4, Cl: 1, P: 3 (can be 5).
Polar CovalentUnequal sharing of electrons due to electronegativity difference. More electronegative atom pulls electron density towards itself → partial charges (δ+ and δ−). Example: H–Cl, H–F, H–O.
Exam Q & A
Q1. Explain the formation of calcium chloride (CaCl₂) with electron diagrams. What type of bond does it have?+
Ionic bond (metal Ca + non-metal Cl).

Ca (Z=20): configuration 2,8,8,2 → loses 2 electrons → Ca²⁺ (2,8,8)
Cl (Z=17): configuration 2,8,7 → gains 1 electron → Cl⁻ (2,8,8)

Since Ca loses 2 electrons, it needs 2 Cl atoms (each taking 1 electron):
Ca → Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻
Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻ (× 2)
Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → CaCl₂

The Ca²⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are held by electrostatic attraction = ionic bond. CaCl₂ has high melting point, soluble in water, conducts electricity in solution.
Q2. What is a coordinate bond? How is it formed in the ammonium ion (NH₄⁺)?+
Coordinate bond: A covalent bond in which both electrons of the shared pair come from one atom alone (the donor).

Formation of NH₄⁺:
1. In NH₃, nitrogen has 3 N–H covalent bonds and 1 lone pair.
2. When HCl (or any acid) provides H⁺ (which has an empty orbital/shell), the lone pair on N is donated to H⁺.
3. N → H (coordinate bond) is formed. The resulting NH₄⁺ has 4 equivalent N–H bonds (the original 3 covalent + 1 coordinate, but once formed, all 4 are indistinguishable).

The compound formed is NH₄Cl (ammonium chloride), which is ionic (NH₄⁺ and Cl⁻).

Properties of Acids

🧪 Acids — Properties

  • Sour taste (e.g., citric acid in lemon, acetic acid in vinegar)
  • Turn blue litmus red; turn methyl orange red/pink; turn phenolphthalein colourless
  • React with metals → salt + hydrogen gas: Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
  • React with metal oxides → salt + water (neutralisation)
  • React with metal carbonates → salt + water + CO₂: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑
  • React with bases → salt + water (neutralisation): HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
  • Conduct electricity (electrolytes in solution) — due to H⁺ ions
  • pH < 7 (neutral pH = 7, acidic pH < 7, alkaline pH > 7)

🔑 How to Distinguish Similar Ions

Al³⁺ vs Zn²⁺ vs Pb²⁺:

Empirical & Molecular Formula
Empirical FormulaThe simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound. Example: CH₂O (for glucose, which is C₆H₁₂O₆).
Molecular FormulaThe actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule. = n × empirical formula, where n = Mᵣ(molecular) / Mᵣ(empirical).

🔢 Steps to Find Empirical Formula

  1. Write percentage composition of each element
  2. Divide each % by atomic mass → mole ratio
  3. Divide all by the smallest mole ratio → simplest ratio
  4. If any ratio is not whole number, multiply all to get whole numbers
  5. Write empirical formula from simplest ratio
  6. Calculate empirical formula mass; divide given Mᵣ by it → n; multiply EF by n → MF

Example: Compound: 40% C, 6.67% H, 53.33% O. Moles: C=40/12=3.33, H=6.67/1=6.67, O=53.33/16=3.33. Divide by 3.33: C:H:O = 1:2:1 → Empirical formula = CH₂O. If Mᵣ = 180: n = 180/30 = 6 → Molecular formula = C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose).

Gay-Lussac's Law of Combining Volumes & Avogadro's Law

📐 Gay-Lussac's Law

Gases combine in simple whole-number ratios by volume (at same temperature and pressure).

Example: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl (1 vol + 1 vol → 2 vol)

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (1 vol + 3 vol → 2 vol)

📐 Avogadro's Law

Equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules.

∴ Volume ratio = mole ratio (for gases at same T, P). This is why stoichiometric coefficients can be used directly as volume ratios for gaseous reactions.

Electrolysis

Core Definitions
ElectrolysisThe process of decomposing an electrolyte (in molten or dissolved form) by passing a direct electric current through it.
ElectrolyteA substance that conducts electricity in molten state or aqueous solution by the movement of ions. Examples: NaCl, CuSO₄, H₂SO₄, NaOH. Non-electrolytes: sugar, urea (no ions).
AnodePositive electrode. Connected to positive terminal of battery. Anions (negative ions) are attracted to anode → lose electrons (oxidation) at anode. ANOX (ANOde = OXidation).
CathodeNegative electrode. Cations (positive ions) attracted to cathode → gain electrons (reduction) at cathode. CARED (CAthode = REDuction).
Preferential DischargeWhen multiple ions are present, the ion discharged at each electrode depends on: (1) position in electrochemical series (ions lower in series discharge first at cathode; higher discharge at anode), (2) concentration of ions, (3) nature of electrodes.
Electrolysis of Different Electrolytes

⚡ Electrolysis of Dilute H₂SO₄ (Platinum Electrodes)

Cathode (reduction): H⁺ ions from H₂SO₄ and H₂O are attracted. H⁺ is discharged preferentially.
2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂↑
Hydrogen gas evolved at cathode.

Anode (oxidation): OH⁻ ions from water discharged (SO₄²⁻ needs too much energy).
4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ + 4e⁻
Oxygen gas evolved at anode. H₂:O₂ = 2:1 by volume.

Overall effect: Water is decomposed; H₂SO₄ concentration increases.

⚡ Electrolysis of CuSO₄ (Copper Electrodes — Electroplating setup)

Cathode: Cu²⁺ ions discharged.
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Copper deposits on cathode (pink, shiny copper).

Anode (copper): Copper anode dissolves.
Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻
Anode loses mass; cathode gains mass. CuSO₄ concentration stays constant.

Application: Electroplating and electrorefining of copper. The impure copper is the anode; pure copper is the cathode. Impurities (Au, Ag) fall as anode mud.

⚡ Electrolysis of CuSO₄ (Platinum Electrodes)

Cathode: Cu²⁺ discharged → copper deposits.
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Anode (Pt — inert): OH⁻ discharged → oxygen evolved.
4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂↑ + 4e⁻
CuSO₄ solution becomes dilute as Cu²⁺ is removed.

⚡ Electrolysis of Brine (Concentrated NaCl solution)

Cathode: H⁺ (from water) discharged (Na⁺ stays in solution — too high in electrochemical series).
2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂↑

Anode: Cl⁻ discharged preferentially (high concentration) over OH⁻.
2Cl⁻ → Cl₂↑ + 2e⁻

Products: H₂ (cathode), Cl₂ (anode), NaOH (remains in solution). These 3 products are the basis of the chlor-alkali industry — used to make PVC, bleach, caustic soda, HCl.

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

📏 Faraday's First Law

The mass of substance deposited or liberated at an electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed.

m ∝ Q = I × t
m = mass deposited (g), Q = charge (Coulombs), I = current (A), t = time (s)

📏 Faraday's Second Law

When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the masses of different substances deposited are proportional to their equivalent masses.

m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂
E = equivalent mass = Molar mass / valency. For Cu²⁺: E = 64/2 = 32 g. For Ag⁺: E = 108/1 = 108 g.
Quantitative Calculation
m = (M × I × t) / (n × 96500)
M = molar mass (g/mol), n = charge on ion (valency), 96500 C = 1 Faraday = charge of 1 mol electrons. Or: m = (E × Q) / 96500 where E = equivalent mass.

🔥 Blast Furnace — Extraction of Iron

Exam Q & A
Q1. Give the role of coke, limestone, and hot air blast in the blast furnace.+
Coke (Carbon): Serves two roles: (1) Fuel — burns in air blast to generate heat (~1500°C). (2) Reducing agent — CO₂ formed reacts with more coke at higher temperatures to give CO (C + CO₂ → 2CO). This CO then reduces iron ore: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂.

Limestone (CaCO₃): Acts as a basic flux. It decomposes at high temperatures: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. The CaO then reacts with the acidic gangue (SiO₂): CaO + SiO₂ → CaSiO₃ (slag). Slag is lower density than iron → floats → can be tapped off separately.

Hot air blast: Supplies oxygen to burn coke (C + O₂ → CO₂) — generates the intense heat (~1500°C) needed to melt iron and create reducing conditions.
Q2. Why must aluminium be extracted by electrolysis and not by reduction with coke?+
Aluminium is a very reactive metal — it is high in the reactivity (electrochemical) series, above carbon. For reduction to work, the reducing agent must be more reactive than the metal being extracted.

Carbon (coke) is less reactive than aluminium. Therefore, carbon cannot displace aluminium from its oxide — the reaction Al₂O₃ + 3C → 2Al + 3CO does NOT occur under normal industrial conditions. The bond between Al and O is too strong for carbon to break.

Therefore, electrical energy (electrolysis) must be used to force the decomposition of molten alumina. This requires huge amounts of electricity, making aluminium production expensive — which is why aluminium recycling is very important economically.

Exam Q & A
Q1. Give the reactions at each electrode in the Hall-Héroult process. Why are the anodes replaced periodically?+
Cathode reaction (reduction):
Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (liquid aluminium forms and collects at bottom of tank)

Anode reaction (oxidation):
2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ (oxygen is produced at anode)

Why anodes are replaced: The carbon (graphite) anodes are consumed because the oxygen produced at the anode reacts with the hot carbon:
C + O₂ → CO₂
This oxidation of carbon gradually burns away the anode. The anodes therefore decrease in size during the process and must be regularly replaced (lowered and eventually replaced entirely) to maintain contact with the electrolyte. This adds to the cost of aluminium production.
Q2. Why is aluminium more expensive to extract than iron, even though aluminium is more abundant in the Earth's crust?+
Aluminium is more expensive to extract than iron for several reasons:

1. Method of extraction: Aluminium is extracted by electrolysis (Hall-Héroult), which requires a continuous supply of large amounts of electricity. Iron is extracted by the blast furnace using cheaper coke as the reducing agent — a simpler, less expensive process.

2. Aluminium's position in the activity series: Al is higher than C in the electrochemical/activity series — carbon cannot reduce Al₂O₃. Electrolysis is the only viable method.

3. Energy requirements: Melting and maintaining the electrolyte at ~950°C plus providing the electrical energy for electrolysis makes the process energy-intensive and costly.

4. Anode replacement: Carbon anodes are consumed and must be replaced regularly, adding to operational costs.

Despite this, aluminium's light weight, corrosion resistance, and recyclability make it economically important.

Exam Q & A
Q1. Why does dry HCl gas not affect dry litmus paper, but moist HCl does?+
HCl is an ionic compound in solution but exists as polar covalent molecules in the pure (dry) state.

Dry HCl: HCl molecules cannot ionise without water. No H⁺ ions are produced. Since litmus responds to H⁺ ions (acid), dry litmus is unaffected by dry HCl gas.

Moist HCl: In the presence of water, HCl ionises completely (strong acid): HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻. The H₃O⁺ (H⁺) ions produced make the solution acidic → turn blue litmus red.

This demonstrates that water is essential for ionisation and acidic properties of HCl.
Q2. How is HCl collected and why not over water?+
HCl is collected by downward displacement of air (upward delivery tube into an inverted jar).

Why not over water? HCl is extremely soluble in water (about 442 volumes dissolve in 1 volume water at 0°C). If collected over water, the gas would immediately dissolve in the water and not collect as a gas. Therefore, water displacement is impossible.

HCl can be collected downward because it is denser than air (molar mass of HCl = 36.5 g/mol vs air ≈ 29 g/mol), so it sinks and fills the jar from the bottom upward.

Exam Q & A
Q1. State the conditions for the Haber process. Why is 450°C used instead of a higher temperature?+
Conditions: Temperature 450–500°C, Pressure 150–200 atm, Catalyst: Iron (Fe) with promoters (Al₂O₃, K₂O).

Why not higher temperature? The Haber process is exothermic (ΔH = −92 kJ/mol). By Le Chatelier's Principle, increasing temperature shifts equilibrium to the left (endothermic direction) → less NH₃ produced → lower equilibrium yield.

However, at very low temperatures, the rate of reaction is too slow to be economically viable.

450°C is a compromise: Gives an acceptable rate of reaction (reasonable speed) while still producing an acceptable yield of NH₃ (~15%). The unreacted gases are recycled, so overall efficiency is high despite low single-pass conversion.
Q2. Write the equation for the reaction of ammonia with copper oxide. What type of reaction is this?+
2NH₃ + 3CuO → 3Cu + 3H₂O + N₂

This is a redox reaction (oxidation-reduction):
• NH₃ is the reducing agent — it reduces black copper(II) oxide to copper metal (pink/reddish). NH₃ itself is oxidised (N goes from −3 in NH₃ to 0 in N₂).
• CuO is the oxidising agent — Cu²⁺ is reduced to Cu⁰.

Observation: Black CuO turns pink/copper coloured; water droplets appear on cool parts; gas (N₂) is evolved (colourless, no smell, extinguishes burning splint). This reaction shows NH₃ acts as a reducing agent.

Q1. Explain the action of conc. H₂SO₄ on sugar with equation. What does this demonstrate?+

Q2. Why does dilute H₂SO₄ react with iron to give FeSO₄ but not Fe₂(SO₄)₃? What does hot conc. H₂SO₄ give?+

Exam Q & A
Q1. How would you distinguish between ethane and ethene using a chemical test? Give equation.+
Test: Pass each gas through bromine water (orange/reddish-brown Br₂ solution).

Ethane (C₂H₆ — saturated, alkane): No reaction in the dark. Bromine water retains its colour (may decolourise slowly in UV light — substitution, but this is very slow under normal conditions). No immediate decolouration.

Ethene (C₂H₄ — unsaturated, alkene): Immediately decolourises bromine water even in the dark — addition reaction:
CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ → CH₂Br–CH₂Br
(1,2-dibromoethane — colourless liquid)

The immediate decolouration of bromine water confirms the presence of a C=C double bond (unsaturation).
Q2. Write the equation for fermentation of glucose. State the conditions. What is produced along with ethanol?+
C₆H₁₂O₆ →(yeast, ~37°C) 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂↑

Conditions:
1. Presence of yeast (contains enzyme zymase — biological catalyst)
2. Temperature ~30–37°C (optimum for enzyme activity; too cold: slow; too hot: enzyme denatured)
3. Absence of air/oxygen (anaerobic conditions) — oxygen would oxidise ethanol to acetic acid
4. pH ~4–6 (slightly acidic)

Products: Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) and carbon dioxide (CO₂) gas.

CO₂ can be detected: by passing through lime water → turns milky (CaCO₃ precipitate). This also confirms fermentation is occurring.
Q3. What are the properties of a homologous series? Give any one example.+
Properties of a Homologous Series:
1. All members have the same general formula.
2. Each member differs from the next by –CH₂– (14 mass units).
3. All members have the same functional group → similar chemical properties.
4. Physical properties (boiling point, melting point, density) change gradually and regularly along the series.
5. All members can be prepared by the same general methods.
6. Members can be represented by a single general formula.

Example — Alcohols (–OH functional group):
Methanol (CH₃OH, M=32) → Ethanol (C₂H₅OH, M=46) → Propanol (C₃H₇OH, M=60) → Butanol (C₄H₉OH, M=74).
Each successive member has one more CH₂ group (+14 mass units). All react with sodium, dehydrate with hot H₂SO₄, form esters with acids, etc.
IUPAC Nomenclature — Rules

📛 Naming Rules (IUPAC)

  1. Find the longest carbon chain (parent chain → gives prefix: 1=meth, 2=eth, 3=prop, 4=but, 5=pent)
  2. Identify functional group → gives suffix (alkane: -ane; alkene: -ene; alkyne: -yne; alcohol: -ol; aldehyde: -al; carboxylic acid: -oic acid)
  3. Number the chain from the end nearer to the functional group (give functional group the lowest possible number)
  4. Name substituents with their position numbers (e.g., 2-methylpropane)
  5. Trivial names still accepted in ICSE: acetic acid (ethanoic), formic acid (methanoic), acetylene (ethyne), ethylene (ethene)

Exam Q & A (Additional)
Q4. What is the difference between n-butane and isobutane? Draw their structural formulae.+
Both have molecular formula C₄H₁₀ — they are structural (chain) isomers.

n-Butane (straight chain):
CH₃–CH₂–CH₂–CH₃ (4 carbons in a straight/unbranched chain, boiling point: −1°C)

Isobutane (branched chain):
CH₃–CH(CH₃)–CH₃ or (CH₃)₃CH (a central carbon with 3 methyl groups, IUPAC: 2-methylpropane, bp: −12°C)

Isobutane has a lower boiling point because the branched structure reduces surface area → weaker van der Waals forces between molecules. Both have same chemical formula but different physical and slightly different chemical properties.
Q5. Explain the preparation of ethyne from calcium carbide. Write the equation. What are its uses?+
Preparation of ethyne (acetylene):
Calcium carbide (CaC₂) is placed in a flask and water is added drop by drop (or Kipp's apparatus is used):
CaC₂ + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + C₂H₂↑

Gas produced: Ethyne (C₂H₂) — colourless gas with characteristic garlic-like odour when impure. Collected by downward displacement of water (or upward displacement of air).

Uses of Ethyne:
1. Oxy-acetylene welding: C₂H₂ burned in O₂ gives a flame ~3500°C — hot enough to cut/weld metals.
2. Making acetic acid (industrial).
3. Making vinyl chloride → PVC plastic.
4. Fruit ripening agent (ethylene generated as a by-product).
